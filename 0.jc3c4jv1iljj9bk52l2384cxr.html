Corporate Influence

Ways in which companies can influence government, and tools to combat that influence: 

  • Commenting on draft regulations
    • Docket Wrench (http://sunlightfoundation.com/tools/docket-wrench/) by the Sunlight Foundation helps groups investigate the regulatory process. In particular, it finds similarly-worded public comments to enable investigators to identify the lobbies among the companies submitting comments. Watch the video for more information.
  • Asset disclosure
    •  tracks each politicians progress in making the required disclosures
  • Disclosure of lobbyist meetings
    • http://quienmanda.es/ by Civio tags politicians and businesspeople in public photographs to annotate which companies are lobbying politicians. Spain has little to no regulation of lobbying. The website serves as both a transparency and a campaign tool to introduce lobbying regulation.
  • Political financing
    • http://moneypoliticstransparency.org/ d 
  • Procurement
    • http://ville.montreal.qc.ca/vuesurlescontrats/ or http://ffctn.com/clients/vdm/contrats/M17/ d 
  • Bribes

  • Dark money, which refers to undeclared donations/contributions to politicians or parties

  • Revolving door (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolving_door_%28politics%29), which is one of the ways in which industries may promote deregulation and privatization
    • http://littlesis.org/ in projects like this, the database maintains and increases in value over time, so it is a good, long-term investment.
  • Ideological hegemony across politics, industry and the media
    • e.g. via the influence of chaebol

Different strategies for combating influence:

  • Lobby for new laws
  • Help enforce existing laws, e.g. via citizen monitoring
  • Create good PR for good politicians and/or bad PR for bad politicians 
  • Support the media, e.g. by making it easier to investigate by providing them with government information in more accessible, searchable, or usable formats

In the session, we described some of the challenges in Korea:

  • Chilling effects (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilling_effect). The mainstream media may lose advertising revenue for covering corruption. (There is more coverage by the independent media.) Academics may lose research funding for investigating corporate influence.
  • The judicial system has a bias towards the status quo and established interests, and is not as strong on defending social justice.

Other notes from the session:

  • http://www.peoplepower21.org/ People’s Solidarity for Participatory Democracy (PSPD) monitors the legislature. Its staff/membership is largely made up of professors, researchers, and journalists. It does regular reporting, but no crowdsourcing.
